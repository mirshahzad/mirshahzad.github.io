Why ISO 27001 implementations fail — and what to do instead
Most organizations treat ISO 27001 as a documentation exercise. They produce policies, tick boxes, and call it done. Then the auditor arrives and nothing holds together. ISO 27001 is not about documents — it is about building a system that actually manages risk. The documentation is evidence of the system, not the system itself.

The vCISO model is broken. Here is what good looks like.
Too many virtual CISOs deliver monthly reports and call it security leadership. Real vCISO work means owning the outcome — not just advising on it. #CyberWithShahzad · Compliance
SOC 2 vs ISO 27001 — which one does your organization actually need?
They are not interchangeable, and choosing the wrong one wastes months of effort. ISO 27001 is a management system standard — it tells the world you manage information security systematically. SOC 2 is an audit report — it tells specific clients that your controls meet defined criteria. Your market decides which one you need, not your preference.

#CyberWithShahzad · Forensics
What 19 forensic investigations taught me about how breaches actually happen
After 19+ forensic investigations across different sectors, the pattern is consistent. Breaches rarely happen because of sophisticated attacks. They happen because of access that was never revoked, accounts that were never monitored, and alerts that nobody acted on. The most advanced threat is usually a former employee with a still-active account.

The Azure security mistake most organizations make on day one
The most common Azure security mistake is not a misconfiguration — it is a default. Microsoft's default settings are designed for usability, not security. Every organization that migrates to Azure without a deliberate security review is inheriting Microsoft's usability defaults as their security posture. That is not a posture. It is an exposure.

#CyberWithShahzad · Leadership
Security is a leadership problem, not a technology problem
Every organization that has suffered a serious breach had some technology in place. Firewalls, antivirus, maybe even a SIEM. What they lacked was not technology — it was a culture where security was owned by leadership, not delegated to IT. The CISO's job is not to configure tools. It is to make security a business priority. Everything else follows from that.
